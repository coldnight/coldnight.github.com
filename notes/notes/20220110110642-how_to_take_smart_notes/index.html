How to Take Smart Notes

2022-01-10 :: [Gray King]
  • tags: Note-Taking,How to Write
  • source: Ahrens, Sönke. How to Take Smart Notes: One Simple Technique to Boost Writing, Learning and Thinking: For Students, Academics and Nonfiction Book Writers. North Charleston, SC: CreateSpace, 2017.

Fleetings⌗

Chapter 01⌗

“I never force myself to do anything I don’t feel like. Whenever I am stuck, I do something else.” – The son of a brewer, most productive and reverd social sientists of the 20^th century.

  • WHAT is the flow?

    It is the state in which you get so completely immersed in you work that you lose track of time time and can just on going as the work becomes effortless.

  • HOW to enable the flow?

    A Good structure enables flow. It’s completely different from make plans about something.

  • WHICH is more helpful to enable the flow? a clear structure or making plans?

    A clear structure is better. Having a clear structure to work in is completely different from making plans about something.

  • WHY a clear structure is better than making plans for enabling the flow?

    • Making a plan makes you inflexible: you impose a structure on yourself, push yourself and employ willpower.
    • Plans will not make you an expert in the learning,writing,note-taking.

Good Solutions are Simple - and Unexpected⌗

Complexity is an issue.

Two well-known and proven ideas.

  • The simple slip-box.

  • Overarching workflow from GTD: Everyting need to be taken care of.

    Even the best tool will not improve your productivity considerably if you don’t change your daily routes the tool is embedded in, just as the fastest car won’t help you much if you don’t have proper roads to drive it on.

GTD the good and the bad:

  • The good
    • Collecting and take care of everyting we need.
    • “mind like water” - the state where we can focus on the work right in front of us without getting distracted by competing thoughts.
    • Providing a structure for our everyday work.
  • The bad
    • Cannot simply be transfered to the task of insightful writing, as a) GTD relies on clearly defined objectives, b) GTD requires projects to be broken down into smaller, concrete “next steps”.

Even the best tools won’t make much of a difference if they are used in isolation. Only if they are embedded in a well-conceived working process can the tools play out their strengths. There is no point in having great tools if they don’t fit together.

The Slip-box⌗

  • WHAT is the slip-box? A unique technique, an external system to think in and organise your thoughts, ideas and collected facts.

  • WHEN is the slip-box developed and published? Developed early in Luhman’s career, and talked about in 1985.

    From as early as 1985, his standard answer to the question of how anyone could be so productive was: “I, of course, do not think everything by myself. It happens mainly within the slip-box” (Luhmann, Baecker, and Stanitzek 1987, 142).

  • WHO developed the slip-box? Niklas Luhman, a German. First went a law school and been a public servant, then go to a newly founded University of Bielefeld as a professor of sociology.

  • HOW does the slip-box work?

    Two slip-boxes:

    • bibliographic one: fleeting notes.
    • main slip-box: shortly after bibliographic notes were taked, review notes in bibliographic slip-box and write down some new.

    An abstract way of giving notes fixed numbers instead organise notes by topic.

  • WHY is the slip-box so unique?

    Because it’s simple and powerful.

The best way to maintain the feeling of being in control is to stay in control.
